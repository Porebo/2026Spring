## Major Lecture Sections

### 1. Why Ethics, Law, and Sustainability Matter in Data Science
Dr. Pierce opened by emphasizing that modern data science is not just technical work. Large-scale data collection, AI systems, cloud infrastructure, and data centers raise difficult questions about privacy, security, sustainability, education, and jobs. She noted that these issues are active in the news and increasingly shape how organizations design and govern technology.

### 2. Privacy, Surveillance, and Data Ownership
The class explored how modern systems collect personal data far more easily than in the past. Public records, APIs, IoT devices, security cameras, traffic cameras, insurance telematics, and smart devices all contribute to a much larger digital footprint.

Key privacy questions discussed:
- What data is being collected about us, including data we do not realize is being captured?
- How is the data being used, and for how long is it retained?
- Who owns the data, and can individuals reclaim or delete it?
- How do we balance benefits such as smart cities, safer streets, and traffic optimization against surveillance risks and misuse?

Dr. Pierce framed privacy as partly a question of consent and comfort: data use can become problematic even without a security breach if organizations use information in ways people would not have agreed to had they fully understood the intended use.

### 3. Security vs. Privacy
The lecture distinguished **security** from **privacy**:

- **Security** concerns unauthorized access to data or systems.
- **Privacy** concerns whether data is being used in ways that violate consent, expectations, or personal boundaries.

Important takeaway: a security failure often creates a privacy problem, but privacy violations can also occur even when a company securely controls the data. The class also reviewed common breach causes such as accidents, social engineering, and insiders, along with the importance of backup, recovery, storage location, and regulatory accountability.

### 4. AI Bias and Discrimination
Bias in AI was a major focus. Since machine learning systems learn from historical data, they can reproduce or amplify existing inequalities. Dr. Pierce reviewed examples involving:

- Resume screening and hiring discrimination
- Medical diagnosis and treatment disparities
- Gender bias in social service summaries
- Hairstyle and professionalism scoring bias
- Credit limit disparities
- Image generation stereotypes for jobs like CEO, nurse, and housekeeper

The class discussed how models may infer protected characteristics indirectly through proxy variables such as school attended, ZIP code, medical history, or patterns associated with race, gender, or age. This makes fairness testing difficult even when those attributes are not explicitly included.

Dr. Pierce also highlighted the challenge of explainability: many models can generate predictions, but it may be difficult to explain clearly how they arrived at a decision. This creates legal and ethical problems, especially in hiring, healthcare, criminal justice, and lending.

### 5. Case Studies and Research Examples
Several recent examples were discussed to illustrate the seriousness of AI bias:

- Large language models portraying older women as less experienced than older men in resume generation and evaluation
- Skin cancer detection models performing poorly on darker skin tones because of unrepresentative training data
- AI-generated care summaries assigning greater need to men than women with similar conditions
- Workday facing legal action over alleged age, race, and disability discrimination in AI-driven hiring
- Large language models producing weaker treatment recommendations when a patient was identified or inferred as African American

These examples reinforced the importance of diverse datasets, systematic bias testing, and research into tools and methods for bias detection and mitigation.

### 6. Privacy Regulation and the CCPA
Dr. Pierce reviewed the **California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA)** and why it became a major turning point in U.S. privacy compliance. Because California is such a large economy, businesses outside California may still be subject to the law if they do business with California residents.

Major CCPA rights discussed:
- Right to know what personal data is collected, used, shared, or sold
- Right to request deletion, within legal and operational limits
- Right to stop the sale or sharing of personal data
- Right to correct inaccurate information
- Right to limit use of sensitive personal information
- Right to non-discrimination for exercising privacy rights
- Special protections for minors
- Ability to pursue legal action after certain breaches

The lecture also covered newer California regulations related to cybersecurity audits, risk assessments, automated decision-making technology, and phased compliance deadlines. Dr. Pierce noted that many states now have similar privacy laws, making privacy governance an increasingly important organizational capability.

### 7. Responsible Student Use of AI
The class reviewed practical guidance for students using AI tools. Appropriate uses include:

- Brainstorming and getting started on assignments
- Improving grammar and writing quality
- Reframing explanations for different audiences
- Exploring tools designed for literature review and research support

However, students should not ask AI to complete assignments for them. Dr. Pierce stressed that outsourcing the work means outsourcing the learning. She also reminded students to use the right tool for the task and to verify outputs carefully because of hallucinations and bias.

Examples of research-support tools mentioned:
- Elicit
- Research Rabbit
- Consensus
- Scispace
- Litmaps
- Scribbr resources for AI-assisted writing and study support

### 8. Employee AI Use, Data Security, and Workplace Policy
The lecture then shifted from student guidance to workplace expectations. Dr. Pierce used University of Richmond staff guidance as an example of how organizations are developing AI policies for employees.

The central principle: employees must not place confidential, restricted, or internal-use data into public AI systems. Examples of sensitive data that should not be entered into public generative AI tools include:

- Personally identifiable information
- Health records
- Financial data
- Student records
- Employment records

Employees should understand:
- Which AI tools are approved by the organization
- Which use cases are acceptable
- When to check with a manager before trying a new AI-supported workflow
- That they remain responsible for the accuracy, legality, and quality of work produced with AI assistance

The lecture also touched on copyright, transparency, non-discrimination, consent, and the environmental cost of generative AI.

## Key Takeaways
1. Ethical, legal, and sustainability issues are central to real-world data science practice, not side topics.
2. Privacy and security are related but distinct; organizations can violate privacy even without a breach.
3. AI bias can appear in hiring, healthcare, lending, image generation, and other high-stakes domains, often through proxy variables.
4. Explainability, fairness testing, and inclusive datasets are essential for responsible AI deployment.
5. CCPA and similar state privacy laws are reshaping how organizations manage data governance and compliance.
6. Students and employees alike can use AI productively, but only with strong verification, privacy awareness, and clear boundaries around sensitive data.
