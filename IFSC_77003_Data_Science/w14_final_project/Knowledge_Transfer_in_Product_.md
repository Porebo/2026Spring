Knowledge transfer literature helps clarify an important distinction between tacit and explicit knowledge that is highly relevant to this project. Schreiber et al. (2011) explain that explicit knowledge can be codified, communicated systematically, and processed through formal tools and information systems, while tacit knowledge is more intuitive, experience-based, context-specific, and difficult to formalize. This distinction is useful because it provides an academic way to describe what workers often call tribal knowledge. In organizational settings, tacit knowledge often moves through personal interaction, observation, and mentoring, whereas explicit knowledge can be stored in documents, repositories, databases, and other structured artifacts (Schreiber et al., 2011).

In the context of this project, mentorship at Aera Energy can be understood as a mechanism for transferring tacit knowledge, while Tableau reports can be understood as artifacts through which portions of that knowledge were codified into explicit organizational knowledge. The dashboards did not necessarily capture every aspect of expert judgment, but they preserved important elements of professional expertise in the form of SQL queries, formulas, filters, visual structures, and data relationships. This makes the Tableau-to-Spotfire transition more than a software migration issue. It becomes a knowledge-transfer and knowledge-preservation problem in which the organization must avoid losing the explicit layer of expertise that had already been encoded into reports, while also recognizing that some tacit understanding may still require human interpretation and organizational absorption (Schreiber et al., 2011).
