AI should not be treated as magic automation, especially in complex analytical environments where domain-specific logic, terminology, and data structures matter. Sherman et al. (2025) show that fine-tuned large language models can significantly improve domain-specific analytical tasks by translating natural language into executable Python code more accurately and efficiently than baseline models. In their geospatial case, the fine-tuned model substantially outperformed the untuned baseline, demonstrating that specialized training, structured prompting, and domain grounding can reduce errors and improve the usability of AI for real analytical work. This is important because it suggests that AI becomes most valuable not when assumed to replace expertise automatically, but when shaped to support a clearly defined domain and workflow (Sherman et al., 2025).

In the context of this project, Sherman et al. (2025) support the idea that Tableau-to-Spotfire migration may benefit from domain-aware AI workflows rather than generic AI use. Just as geospatial analysis required a fine-tuned or carefully structured AI process to reliably interpret spatial tasks, Tableau migration may require AI workflows designed to extract SQL, logic, filters, field mappings, dashboard structure, and interaction behavior from workbook artifacts. This strengthens the argument that AI can serve as a practical knowledge-extraction and preservation tool, but only when it is used with sufficient domain awareness, technical grounding, and human oversight. In that sense, the value of AI for this project lies not in magical automatic conversion, but in its ability to assist with specialized analytical reconstruction in a way that is more scalable and efficient than manual analysis alone (Sherman et al., 2025).
