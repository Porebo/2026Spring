Dashboards should not be understood as merely technical assemblies of code, charts, and data connections, because their value also depends on how effectively they transform data into usable knowledge for real users. Cogan et al. (2025) describe dashboard development as an iterative, user-centered process in which stakeholder needs shape what information is shown, how it is organized, and how it is interpreted. In their study, dashboards were designed not simply to display data, but to communicate progress clearly, support shared understanding among different user groups, and convert practice data into shareable knowledge. This perspective is important because it shows that dashboard meaning is produced not only by backend logic, but also by the visual, interpretive, and workflow choices that make information understandable and actionable for users (Cogan et al., 2025).

In the context of this project, Cogan et al. (2025) support the argument that Tableau dashboards at Aera Energy were more than coded reporting artifacts. They were visual knowledge structures shaped by the expertise of engineers, geologists, technicians, and analysts who knew what information mattered, how it should be arranged, and how it should be interpreted in operational settings. This means that the professional expertise embedded in a dashboard is carried not only in SQL queries and formulas, but also in the visual setup that guides user attention, interpretation, and decision-making. As a result, migration from Tableau to Spotfire should be treated not only as technical reconstruction, but also as the preservation of professional knowledge encoded in the dashboard's design, layout, and interaction patterns (Cogan et al., 2025).
