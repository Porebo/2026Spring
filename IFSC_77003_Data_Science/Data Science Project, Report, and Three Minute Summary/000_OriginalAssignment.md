Data Science Project, Report, and Three Minute Summary: Activity and Assessment
For your semester project, you will be demonstrating your data literacy abilities by conducting a data science investigation, developing a well-written report, and a 3-minute video presentation to share your investigation with others.

Graduate students should aim for a 10–20-page paper (e.g., 12 pt Font, single spacing - 1.15-, and 1-inch margins). Longer papers are fine. Undergraduates should follow the same guidelines as graduate students, but their grading will be curved accordingly.
Each person needs to do their own project. Students can have coordinated projects (i.e., 3 individuals doing different parts of a larger project). The key thing with coordinated projects is that each individual will be graded separately for their section of the overall project work, their own report, and their own 3-minute presentation, so each person must put in a good effort.  Typically for a top rated paper, you want to put in 40 to 60 hours on your final project.
If you need a report template, check out the library or web for examples of published papers (your literature review should help as well). Finally, reports can be submitted in a variety of formats, including Word Document, PDF, or Jupyter/Google Co-lab Notebook. Check with Dr. Pierce before submitting other formats.  
In addition to the project and paper/report itself, students will also do a final presentation (3-minute thesis). The University of Sussex also has some useful tips for preparing a good 3-minute talk.


Once you have selected your topic, identified data science objectives/questions, collected your data, and conducted your analysis, you will need to develop a good report and presentation. Your report for your semester project should follow this format.



PART 1: Title Page or Title Section containing the proposed Title of your project, your name, your supervisor’s name, your institution and department, and Course Number and Name, and the Semester (Date) that you worked on the project.



PART 2: Table of Contents: Depending on the Report, you may or may not need a Table of Contents. It is okay for this class to omit the TOC.



PART 3: Introduction: (Helps provide the motivation for this data science investigation)

Introduce your topic. Who could have an interest in the topic?
Give necessary background and context. How much is already known about the topic? What is missing from this current knowledge?
Outline your problem statement and the research questions. What new insights will your investigation contribute? Why do you believe this project is worth doing?


PART 4: Literature Review (10 to 15 sources) (Provides background and establishes what is known already)

It is important to demonstrate that you are familiar with other data science investigations on your topic. In this section, share how your project will fit into the other conversations about your topic by comparing and contrasting the main theories, methods, and debates. This is also an opportunity to examine the strengths and weaknesses of different approaches or to explain how you build on, challenge, or incorporate prior scholarship.
Useful resources: Guidelines for Writing a Literature Review and Literature Review Steps
Be sure to document that all articles used for your literature review exist. (New with AI) 


PART 5: Data Selection, Research Design, and Methods. (Helps to show that your work is feasible)

Following the literature review, restate your main objectives.
Describe your overall strategy for answering your research questions. (i.e., what data collection procedures, research approaches will you use)
Identify and describe your data sources (e.g., is this a population or sample, where/when does this data come from, are there any data quality issues?)
What data science techniques will you use to analyze the data?


PART 6: Analysis Work - Use this section to present and interpret your analysis of the data.



PART 7: Discussion of Results - Use this section to explain how your results help answer each of your project objectives/questions.  In addition, be sure to describe any security, privacy, or ethical issues that should be considered when doing this type of analysis in a real-world environment.  Also, this is a good place to disclose if and how you used AI to help you with this project.  



PART 8: Conclusions and Closing Thoughts - Use this section to summarize your findings, any contributions or insights that you discovered, any lessons learned, and any thoughts on future work.



PART 9: Appendix

Include your Project Schedule to show a timeline of your project, showing what you did at each stage of the project and how many hours were spent.
Reference List - Include correct citations for every source you have used.
Any additional project documentation that you would like to include to showcase your work?  It is fine to include a copy of your Notebook or Python code (or a link to your folder in the cloud)

Additional 3 Minute Thesis Guidance

Based on the University of Sussex 3MT guidance, the most important points are:

- Your presentation should explain your research topic and why it matters in 3 minutes or less.
- The talk should be designed for a non-specialist audience, so use clear, simple language.
- You may use one single static PowerPoint slide only.
- Do not use slide transitions, animations, audio, video, or other electronic media.
- Do not use props such as costumes, instruments, or equipment.
- The presentation must be spoken word.
- Going over 3 minutes can result in disqualification.
- Strong presentations clearly cover:
  - the background and significance of the research
  - the research strategy, design, or methods
  - the key results or findings
  - the conclusions, outcomes, and impact
- Judges also value:
  - clear delivery
  - an effective slide that supports the talk
  - enthusiasm and audience engagement
