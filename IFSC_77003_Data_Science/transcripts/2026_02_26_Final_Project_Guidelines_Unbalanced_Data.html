IFSC 77003 · Data Science & Technologies · Week 6

Final Project Guidelines, Unbalanced Data Strategies, and Research Reporting

February 26, 2026 Dr. Elizabeth Pierce UALR
Unbalanced Data Project Deliverables Report Structure Literature Review Analysis Expectations Team Coordination

This session connected technical modeling concerns with final-project execution: how to handle rare-event class imbalance, how to structure a strong report, and how to document complete individual contributions in coordinated projects.

Rare Events and Unbalanced Data

The lecture highlighted why raw model accuracy can be deceptive for rare-event problems such as fraud or medical-risk prediction. A model may appear highly accurate while still missing the class that matters most.

StrategyPurposeTypical Tradeoff
Oversampling minority classIncrease rare-class visibility during trainingCan overfit duplicated/synthetic signals
Undersampling majority classReduce class dominancePotential loss of useful majority data
Weighted/penalty lossPenalize rare-class errors more heavilyNeeds careful tuning to avoid instability
Combined resampling + weightingBalance data and objective simultaneouslyMore complex validation workflow
Shared Resource

Students were directed to Dr. Wu's prior bonus materials (recording, slides, notebook, and dataset) for practical workflows on imbalanced-data modeling.

Final Project Deliverables and Scope

  • Complete a full data science investigation.
  • Submit a written report (commonly 10 to 20 pages).
  • Submit a 3-minute video summary of the project.

The project was framed as a major demonstration of data literacy and employability skills, not only assignment completion.

Strong projects show not just output, but clear reasoning: what was tried, what worked, what failed, and why.

Course Guidance

Recommended Report Structure

Core sections

  • Title section with course and project context.
  • Introduction with motivation, problem, and objectives.
  • Literature review with validated sources.
  • Methodology (data access, cleaning, feature/metric design, analysis plan).
  • Analysis with interpretation of outputs.
  • Discussion, conclusions, and future work.
  • Appendix with schedule/hours and full references.

Literature Review Expectations and AI Caution

Students were advised to verify every citation, especially if an LLM is used to brainstorm. Fabricated or mismatched references are common when references are copied without source validation.

Validation Rule

Any source included in the report should be real, accessible, and accurately represented in the literature summary.

Source types can include journal papers, white papers, technical blogs, and notebooks when they are relevant and trustworthy.

Analysis Quality Criteria

  • Explain model and method choices, not just run default pipelines.
  • Describe parameter tuning and evaluation rationale.
  • Use diagnostics such as false positives/false negatives where relevant.
  • Clarify training/testing design and cross-validation choices.
  • Interpret plots/tables in plain language for decision-making.

Coordinated Team Projects and Workload Evidence

Teams may coordinate around a shared dataset and divide work by region or analysis dimension, but each student must still demonstrate complete individual effort and report ownership.

  • Maintain roughly full per-person literature effort (for example, around 10+ sources each).
  • Track individual hours per phase, including peer review and revision.
  • Document coordinated context while preserving separate assessment artifacts.
Practical Reminder

If project data comes from employer or external systems, get explicit permission before submission and protect confidentiality constraints.
