Generative AI — A Closer Look at the Risks

Generative AI — the technology behind ChatGPT, Google Gemini, and Microsoft Copilot — creates original content (text, images, music, code) by learning statistical patterns from massive datasets. Unlike traditional AI that analyzes existing data, generative models produce new material that mimics human output. The 2017 Google Transformer paper remains the foundational advance that made this possible.

Data Privacy

When employees use public generative AI tools, their prompts become part of the model's training corpus. Sensitive information — trade secrets, client details, personnel data — can inadvertently leak outward. On the flip side, one company discovered that their internal ChatGPT deployment had been trained on HR files, allowing anyone to prompt it for individual salaries. Scoping what a model "knows" is a critical and often underestimated deployment challenge.

Hallucinations & Misinformation

Because large language models generate content probabilistically — selecting likely next tokens rather than retrieving verified facts — they can produce responses that look authoritative while being entirely fabricated. This is the AI hallucination problem, and it has real consequences.

Intellectual Property

Traditional copyright law requires human authorship. Content generated by AI currently sits in a legal gray zone: who, if anyone, owns an image produced by Gemini? The company that built Gemini? The user who wrote the prompt? The artists whose work was ingested in training? Courts and regulators worldwide are still working through these questions.

UALR AI Resources & Google Gemini Demo

UALR has developed an AI policy page that includes student resources and links to institutional guidelines. Two tools are available at no cost through university accounts: Google Gemini and Microsoft Copilot. Dr. Pierce also recommended Grammarly as a practical writing companion that catches spelling errors, suggests punctuation improvements, and proposes more concise phrasing — useful for the kind of rapid email drafting that department chairs do constantly.

During a live demo, Google Gemini generated an image of a gray tabby cat climbing an oak tree from a plain text description. Dr. Pierce noted how dramatically image quality has improved over the past 18 months and walked through how prompt engineering adjustments (style: "oil painting," "watercolor," "Dr. Seuss") can shift the output significantly. Students interested in web design may find AI image generation useful for creating original graphics without copyright concerns from stock photography.

How Students Should (and Shouldn't) Use AI

The course is walking a deliberate line: students need to understand AI tools and feel comfortable prompting, brainstorming, and iterating with them — while simultaneously developing their own expertise in their subject domain, which AI currently cannot replace.

Recommended uses

Brainstorming project ideas · Outlining reports · Getting explanations of complex concepts (gradient descent, agentic AI) · Debugging code · Proofreading drafts · Practicing for quizzes via AI-generated questions

Where students get into trouble

Asking AI to write entire assignments without review. Two years ago, a cohort of research-methods students submitted AI-generated research proposals that looked structurally correct but contained hallucinated references — articles that did not exist, or that didn't say what the AI claimed. The proposals were unimplementable. Dr. Wu and Dr. Pierce could not grade them as credible work.

The lesson from the Tilly Norwood case is instructive: she is a fully AI-generated model/influencer who sings, talks, and acts. Creating her required over six months of development and 18 human specialists managing costumes, prompts, editing, movements, and voice. Even at the cutting edge, meaningful AI output demands significant human direction and quality control.

"AI is great at producing things that look like work. Your job is to make sure it actually is work — verified, understood, and defensible."

— lecture paraphrase

Tom Davenport on Citizen Development

Tom Davenport — professor at Babson College and author of more than 200 articles in Harvard Business Review — presented at the Chief Data Officer & Information Quality (CDO IQ) Conference in Boston last summer. Dr. Pierce obtained the session materials through her professional network and shared the highlights.

Davenport's central thesis is the rise of citizen development: AI-powered, low-code tools are enabling non-IT business staff to build applications, automate workflows, and perform data analysis that previously required a dedicated development team.

How Citizen Development Changes the IT Relationship

Historically, a business team with an idea for a new report or application had to schedule time with IT liaisons, explain requirements, wait for development, and iterate slowly. Now, business users can generate a working prototype using low-code tools — letting IT move from requirements-gathering to code review and production hardening. The business user shows up with a blueprint; IT ensures security, privacy, and performance.

Risks of Citizen Development

Citizen tools are not failsafe. Business users may work with stale or inconsistent data, incorrectly transform variables, or encode bad rules into automated decisions. Data scientists and IT specialists remain essential — their role shifts from building everything from scratch to providing governance, quality assurance, and oversight.

AT&T's ML Ops program addresses a specific technical challenge: machine learning models degrade over time ("like a gallon of milk — good for two weeks") and must be updated continuously in live, 24/7 production environments without downtime. This is a new and growing career specialty analogous to the DevOps movement that Amazon pioneered for continuous software deployment.

"I'd rather have someone who truly understands my customers and my business using automated machine learning than a brilliant data scientist who doesn't know my domain."

— Head of Credit Risk, Canadian bank (quoted by Tom Davenport)
Career takeaway

Pair your data science and statistics skills with deep expertise in a specific domain — healthcare, sports analytics, HR, finance, supply chain. That combination is what companies are willing to pay a premium for.

Agentic AI — From Reactive to Proactive

Generative AI is reactive: you prompt it, it responds. Agentic AI is the next step — proactive systems that are given goals and tool access, then independently plan and execute multi-step tasks without a human handling each step.

A February 2026 article in MIT Management Sloan Review offered concrete examples: an AI travel agent that reads a traveler's preferences, queries flight and hotel APIs, communicates via Slack, and with credit card authorization, books the complete itinerary. A warehouse monitoring agent that watches multiple camera feeds and alerts security to anomalies — intruders, water leaks, smoke — without requiring a person to stare at screens. Cybersecurity agents that handle first-level network monitoring, freeing analysts for complex escalations.

The Entry-Level Pipeline Problem

If AI takes over junior-level roles (the entry analyst, the junior banker handling routine loan reviews), how will the next generation develop the experience needed for mid and senior positions? Dr. Pierce proposed one solution: mandate human-AI pairing at entry level — ensuring humans do the work alongside the AI, developing genuine expertise, before advancing.

Artificial General Intelligence — The Theoretical Horizon

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) — AI that is autonomous, highly adaptive, capable of teaching itself, and equal to or exceeding human cognitive ability across domains — remains theoretical. It is distinct from narrow AI (chatbots, image generators) and from agentic AI (task-specific multi-step systems).

How Far Away?

Predictions span a remarkable range. Elon Musk has suggested AGI could arrive within a year — the same source who forecast fully autonomous vehicles by now (still not realized). The mainstream research consensus is 5–10 years. Some researchers say 50–60 years. Dr. Pierce's personal view is that AGI will follow a trajectory similar to autonomous vehicles.

What Makes AGI Hard

Current LLMs predict the next token; AGI would need to reason. Some researchers argue true AGI also requires embodied intelligence: human cognition is shaped by sight, hearing, smell, touch, and taste — inputs that ground our reasoning in physical reality. Until machines can integrate those sensory streams, matching human-level general intelligence may be structurally out of reach.

"We're probably not going to have to worry about the Terminator scenario in our lifetimes."

— Dr. Elizabeth Pierce

AI Ethics — A Rapid Survey

  • Historical bias: Models trained on historically biased data will reproduce and scale those biases. Synthetic data generation is one research avenue to build fairer training sets.
  • Explainability: Regulators require explanations for consequential AI decisions. "We don't know" is categorically unacceptable for loan denials or medical guidance.
  • Autonomy & control: As AI systems take more actions independently, accountability frameworks become critical.
  • Job displacement: Still unfolding; highly dependent on sector and role type.
  • Security & misuse: Deepfakes, disinformation, adversarial attacks on AI systems.
  • High-stakes domains: Healthcare, criminal justice, banking, and housing demand far stronger safeguards than, say, deciding how many widgets to stock.
  • Environmental impact: Training and running large models consumes significant energy; sustainability is a growing focus.
  • Education: How do we ensure students develop genuine expertise when AI can shortcut the learning process? The answer matters for long-term workforce health.
