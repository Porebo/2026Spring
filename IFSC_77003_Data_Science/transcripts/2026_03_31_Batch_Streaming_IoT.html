Batch Processing vs. Streaming Data

The core contrast is between two fundamentally different relationships with data in motion.

Batch Processing — The Data Lake

In batch processing, you collect first, then process. A large body of records accumulates in a repository (the "data lake"), and when you're ready to run your job, you process them all — or break them into sub-batches for parallel processing. The output follows some time later: anywhere from minutes to hours.

Classic example: monthly credit card billing. All transactions from the billing cycle are tabulated at once, and statements are generated en masse. When Dr. Pierce worked at IBM, batch jobs were submitted before 5 pm and results were ready by 9 am the next morning — approximately 16 hours of latency.

COBOL note: COBOL, a language from the 1960s, still holds roughly 13% of the business computing market (down from 85% at its peak) and remains dominant in banking, insurance, and healthcare billing precisely because it excels at exactly these deterministic, rules-based batch calculations. About 60–70% of all healthcare IT billing reportedly still runs on COBOL. It is efficient, reliable, and cheap to operate — qualities that aren't going anywhere.

Streaming Data — The Data River

Streaming inverts the relationship: instead of waiting to accumulate data, you analyze it as it flows by. A continuous feed of events arrives, and your system focuses on whatever falls inside a designated sliding time window — the last 15 readings, the last 5 seconds, the last 30 events. Based on what's in that window, you make a real-time decision.

Example: an equipment temperature sensor reports every 2 seconds. Every 30 seconds, the system averages the last 15 readings. If the average exceeds a threshold, an alarm fires. More sophisticated: if the last five readings are progressively higher (even if the average is still safe), a trend alert fires early — catching problems before they become emergencies.

"You're standing at the bank of the Arkansas River. You can only grab what's floating by you right now — and you have to decide in the moment."

— Dr. Pierce on the streaming data analogy

Real-World Streaming Applications

  • Healthcare — UAMS ER prediction: Weather + time-of-day + day-of-week data (updated every 6 hours) predicted ER surges, triggering staffing calls, bed clearing, and elective surgery postponements.
  • Patient monitoring: Heart rate can go from normal to cardiac event in seconds — streaming windows, not batch jobs, are what save lives.
  • Industrial equipment: Temperature, vibration, and pressure sensors on machinery enable predictive maintenance, preventing costly failures.
  • Smart buildings: Motion sensors, smart meters, HVAC controls, and security systems all feed real-time streams.
  • Smart cities: Traffic feeds, emergency services routing, utility monitoring.
  • Financial markets: Stock price updates, fraud detection, and trading systems operate at microsecond latency.
  • Real-time sports betting (e.g., DraftKings): Live odds updates from real-time game event streams — a rapidly growing and ethically complex application area.
  • Agriculture: Soil moisture and humidity sensors determine when to activate or shut off irrigation — hourly or daily windows are often sufficient.

Choosing Batch vs. Streaming

The choice is not binary — the same underlying data (e.g., social media posts) can be batched for in-depth analysis or streamed for real-time trend monitoring. The decision comes down to: how quickly do you need results? What's the cost of a delayed decision? What infrastructure can you support?

Streaming Data Challenges

Computational Speed

If your analysis cannot complete inside the window, results arrive too late to act on. Systems must be sized to process events faster than they arrive.

Data Quality in Streams

In a batch dataset of a million records, a few bad records are absorbed by the statistical weight of the rest. In a streaming window of 10–15 observations, one or two malfunctioning sensors can be the majority of your data — and can flip a decision from "safe" to "alarm" (or worse, from "alarm" to "safe"). Data quality monitoring in streaming systems is a distinct and demanding discipline.

Sensor malfunction risk

Unlike batch reprocessing — where you can go back to the repository and rerun the job — once a streaming window has passed, that data may be gone. Diagnosing a false alarm from three weeks ago can be impossible without archiving strategies.

Auditability & Replay

If you need to answer "what did the data look like in that window at 2:15 am on a Tuesday three weeks ago?", streaming systems require deliberate snapshot or archiving strategies. Storing periodic snapshots at high frequency quickly becomes a storage challenge. For applications that don't require audit trails (home intruder detection: you only care if someone is there right now), this is not an issue. For healthcare or financial applications, it can be critical.

Security

Just as attackers target data lakes, they also try to intercept or inject into data streams — particularly financial transaction streams or streams carrying personally identifiable information (PII). Any retained streaming data that contains patient information must meet HIPAA compliance; student-related data must meet FERPA standards. The same regulatory burden applies regardless of whether the underlying architecture is batch or streaming.

Scalability

Streaming systems require more memory and processing power than equivalent batch setups. Systems must be horizontally scalable to handle traffic spikes — a streaming architecture that works at 10,000 events/second may not hold at 10 million. With batch, you can always add more computers to a cluster and wait longer; with streaming, "wait longer" defeats the purpose.

Internet of Things — Introduction

The Internet of Things (IoT) is the hardware layer that generates the streaming data discussed above. IoT systems connect physical sensors to cloud platforms via a simple three-component architecture:

Sensor → Power Source → Hotspot/Network → Cloud → Dashboard

Smart Dashboards

Static dashboards visualize what's happening. Smart dashboards apply rules to the incoming stream and send automated alerts (text, email, push notification) when a threshold or pattern is detected. You don't need to watch a screen 24/7 — the system watches for you and only calls when something requires attention.

IoT Demo: ThingsBoard

Dr. Pierce demonstrated ThingsBoard — a company that sells IoT kits including sensors, cloud hosting, and dashboard design software. A basic starter system is free; subscription tiers scale with larger IoT deployments. The platform allows users to design custom dashboards in their cloud account — monitoring anything from energy consumption to environmental conditions to equipment health.

Dr. Phil Williams (UALR bioinformatics) has used similar setups — built on Raspberry Pi hardware — to monitor water salt levels as a research and teaching project. Commercial kits like ThingsBoard's lower the barrier for students who want to explore IoT without custom hardware engineering.

IoT Application Areas

  • Smart homes: Lighting, smart meters, security systems, connected appliances
  • Smart cities: Traffic management, utility monitoring, emergency services coordination
  • Agriculture: Soil moisture, weather station data, automated irrigation control
  • Industrial: Equipment performance monitoring, predictive maintenance, factory floor sensors
  • Healthcare: Patient vital signs, hospital infrastructure (temperature, humidity in labs and pharmacies)
  • Environmental science: Air quality, water quality, ecosystem monitoring
