Ethics, Law, and Sustainability in Data Science

Dr. Pierce framed this lecture as an entry point into a much larger professional landscape. Ethical, legal, and sustainability questions now surround data science, big data infrastructure, and AI in ways that affect engineers, business leaders, policy makers, IT professionals, and researchers alike. The class discussed how these issues now appear constantly in the news because they shape education, employment, public safety, data center expansion, and the long-term social impact of AI systems.

"These topics are not side issues. They shape how data science is built, deployed, regulated, and trusted."

Lecture Theme

Privacy, Surveillance, and Security

The lecture examined how easy large-scale data collection has become. Public records, APIs, Ring cameras, traffic cameras, smart vehicles, insurance tracking apps, and connected devices all contribute to a persistent digital trail. A recurring question was whether people fully understand what is being collected, how long it is retained, and how it is shared.

Key Distinction

Security is about unauthorized access. Privacy is about whether data is used in ways that violate consent, expectations, or comfort, even if the organization technically controls the data securely.

Questions Raised in Class

  • Who owns personal data once it has been collected?
  • Can individuals ask for that data to be deleted or corrected?
  • What counts as acceptable use versus misuse?
  • How do we balance smart-city and public-safety benefits against surveillance risks?
  • Where is the data physically stored, and which jurisdiction governs it?

Dr. Pierce also noted that data breaches are not caused only by elite attackers. Accidents, insiders, and social engineering remain major sources of exposure, which means privacy and security are both technical and organizational challenges.

AI Bias, Proxy Variables, and High-Stakes Decisions

A major portion of the class focused on how AI and machine learning systems can replicate or intensify historical bias. Because models learn from past data, they can absorb distorted patterns tied to race, gender, age, disability, skin tone, and socioeconomic status. Even when a protected attribute is not explicitly included, the model may infer it from proxies such as ZIP code, school attended, purchase behavior, or health history.

Area Example Discussed Why It Matters
Hiring Resume screening and Workday litigation Qualified people may be filtered out because of age, race, gender, or disability-related signals.
Healthcare Lesion detection and treatment recommendation disparities Bias can reduce detection quality or produce weaker care recommendations for some groups.
Social Services AI summaries describing men as more dependent than women with similar needs Bias can distort care allocation and institutional decision-making.
Credit & Finance Women receiving lower credit limits despite stronger profiles Opaque scoring systems can encode unfair treatment at scale.
Image Generation CEO prompts yielding white men; nurse or housekeeper prompts yielding women or minorities Generative AI can reinforce stereotypes in subtle but powerful ways.
Bias Testing Problem

Traditional software testing checks for syntax, runtime, logic, and performance errors. AI adds a harder question: how do we systematically test for unfairness, hidden proxy behavior, and unequal impact across groups?

The lecture also emphasized explainability. A model may produce a prediction, but if an organization cannot explain how a decision was made in understandable terms, that becomes a legal, managerial, and ethical problem, especially in hiring, criminal justice, lending, and healthcare.

CCPA and the Expanding Privacy Regulation Landscape

The class reviewed the California Consumer Privacy Act as a major turning point in U.S. privacy compliance. Its practical importance extends far beyond California because many organizations outside the state still do business with California residents. Dr. Pierce explained that this made privacy governance a nationwide issue for many businesses, including universities and online organizations.

Rights Highlighted in Class

  • Right to know what personal information is collected, used, shared, or sold
  • Right to request deletion, with exceptions for legal or operational obligations
  • Right to stop the sale or sharing of personal information
  • Right to correct inaccurate information
  • Right to limit the use of sensitive personal data
  • Right to non-discrimination for exercising privacy rights
  • Stronger protections for minors
Compliance Reality

Even organizations that think they are outside a specific privacy law may still benefit from aligning with it early, especially as more states adopt similar legislation and consumers expect stronger control over their data.

The lecture also referenced newer California requirements around cybersecurity audits, risk assessments, and automated decision-making technology, along with the fact that many additional states now have similar or emerging privacy laws.

Responsible AI Use for Students and Employees

Dr. Pierce split AI guidance into two contexts: academic use and workplace use. For students, AI can be appropriate for brainstorming, polishing writing, clarifying ideas, and supporting research discovery when used carefully. But asking AI to complete the assignment itself undermines learning and often introduces factual problems through hallucination or poor sourcing.

Student Guidance

  • Use AI to brainstorm, revise, or rephrase, not to replace your own thinking.
  • Prefer specialized research-support tools when doing literature reviews.
  • Verify facts, quotes, and references through reliable search tools and vetted sources.

Employee Guidance

Using the University of Richmond as an example, the lecture showed how organizations are creating AI policies for staff. The most important rule is that employees must not place confidential or restricted data into public AI systems.

  • Do not enter personally identifiable information into public AI tools.
  • Do not submit health, financial, student, or employment records into open systems.
  • Know which AI tools are approved by your organization.
  • Check with a manager when trying a new workplace AI use case.
  • Remain accountable for the accuracy, legality, and quality of the final work.

The lecture also touched on transparency, copyright, consent, non-discrimination, and the environmental cost of generative AI systems.
