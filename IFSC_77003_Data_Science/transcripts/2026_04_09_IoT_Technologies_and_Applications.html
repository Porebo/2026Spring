Origins of IoT: From RFID to a General Paradigm

Dr. Dagtas opened by framing IoT as the convergence of two capabilities: connectivity and self- or environment-awareness through sensors. The term was coined by Kevin Ashton in the late 1990s while working on RFID at MIT. What began as RFID tagging grew, over two decades, into a much broader technological concept.

When devices became addressable at the scale that IPv6 allowed, simple low-power things could finally live on the internet as first-class network participants.

Enabling Shift
The Technologies That Joined RFID

NFC (now used in phone-based payments), visual tagging (barcodes and QR codes), Bluetooth, ZigBee and other wireless sensor network standards, and digital watermarking for security all layered on top of the original RFID foundation.

The promise Dr. Dagtas repeated throughout the lecture: once the physical world generates rich, continuous data, that data can feed AI-style macro decisions across industries — making many processes more efficient and more effective than guesswork or sampling alone.

What Has to Converge for IoT to Work

IoT is not a single technology. It is an integration problem that only becomes viable when several independent advancements arrive at once.

Layer What It Provides Examples
Connectivity How devices reach each other and the internet Bluetooth, ZigBee, Wi-Fi, LTE, 5G, IPv6
Addressability A unique identifier for each device IPv6 addresses, device IDs
Sensing Capturing the state of the world Temperature, light, motion, pulse, camera, microphone
Actuation Acting on the world Open a door, change a traffic light, turn lights on
Embedded processing Local computation on the device itself Microcontrollers running minimal firmware
Cloud and fog Heavier processing, long-term storage, AI AWS / Azure / GCP plus local smart-home servers
Why IPv6 Mattered

IPv4's address space simply cannot support billions of tiny devices. IPv6 is what made it practical to give every sensor, watch, or home device a real IP address rather than forcing them behind NAT gateways.

How IoT Devices Are Actually Built

IoT devices are deliberately small, low-power, and inexpensive so they can be deployed in large numbers. That shapes every design decision.

  • A microcontroller (Dr. Dagtas showed an 8-bit AVR running at 20 MHz, smaller than a quarter) handles onboard logic.
  • Onboard sensors capture the needed measurement: pulse, force, light, motion, and so on.
  • The microcontroller organizes the data, temporarily stores it, and manages communication with a nearby device.
  • Communication typically uses Bluetooth or a low-power wireless sensor network protocol like ZigBee.
  • Mesh networking lets devices relay messages to each other instead of each one talking to a central base station, which drops power usage dramatically.
Battery Life as a First-Class Design Goal

Mesh networking and careful power management are why many sensors can run for months, years, or longer on a single battery. Some deployments add tiny solar panels, but many environments are too dark for that, so power budget discipline is the norm.

Devices Relay Through More Powerful Devices

A smartwatch has more compute and storage than a minimal sensor but far less than a phone. A common pattern: the sensor talks to the watch, the watch talks to the phone, and the phone passes relevant data to the cloud. Different categories of device fill different roles in the relay chain.

The ABCDs of IoT

Dr. Dagtas offered a simple mnemonic for the four critical components of any real IoT application.

Letter Component Role
A Applications The specific domain: healthcare, transportation, retail, agriculture.
B Big data / Analytics Turning raw sensor streams into information, knowledge, and action.
C Connectivity Networking stack from Bluetooth and ZigBee up through LTE and 5G.
D Devices The wearables and embedded sensors out in the world.
Raw Data Alone Is Of Limited Value

Pulse rate as a standalone number does not diagnose anything. Pulse combined with activity data, oxygen saturation, sleep trends, and personal baselines becomes medically meaningful. IoT value lives in the integration, not the individual sensor reading.

Cloud Computing, Fog Nodes, and Device Categories

Cloud computing completes the IoT puzzle by providing storage, deep learning infrastructure, and long-term analytics at a scale that no sensor or phone could match. Cloud services show up in the familiar SaaS / PaaS / IaaS forms introduced in the previous session.

Fog Computing as the Middle Layer

Before data reaches the cloud, it often passes through an intermediate tier. Fog nodes are local servers — sometimes dedicated smart-home hubs like an Alexa-style device — that handle a lot of local processing and only escalate to the cloud when necessary. They act as a bridge: in constant communication with the devices around them, but also capable of talking upstream.

Why Fog Exists

Latency, bandwidth, and power all push some work closer to the sensor. Fog nodes reduce round-trips, reduce upstream data volume, and make the system more responsive for local decisions (like detecting a fire event before a cloud call round-trips).

Six Categories of IoT-Integrated Devices

  1. Category 1: minimal low-end sensing nodes.
  2. Category 2: mesh-network participants with some networking capability.
  3. Category 3: more advanced compute, IP-network capable, can talk to tablets and phones.
  4. Category 4: tablets and mobile phones.
  5. Category 5: PCs and laptops.
  6. Category 6: cloud servers or advanced local servers in a professional setting.

A working IoT deployment typically spans several of these categories, with data flowing up and decisions flowing back down.

20-Year Arc

Many of these applications — smart watches, smart stores, wearable medical sensors — were experimental research topics 15–20 years ago. Dr. Dagtas noted that several projects he personally worked on in that era have since matured into real products and daily practices.

Case Study: Military Sensor Placement Optimization

Dr. Dagtas closed with a project he worked on for the US military involving data fusion across multiple sensor types and optimal sensor placement in a building.

The Placement Problem

  • Different sensor types have different coverage shapes.
  • Motion and light sensors cover roughly circular areas.
  • Visual sensors (cameras) are directional — they cover a cone defined by their angle of view and pointing direction.
  • RF propagation for wireless communication is non-linear and context-dependent.
  • The goal: place the minimum number of each sensor type in the right locations to maximize coverage at minimum total cost while preserving mesh connectivity.
What Made This Interesting

Every building changes the answer. Placement is not a one-time decision — it is an optimization problem that must be re-solved for each environment, balancing sensing coverage, networking reach, total cost, and power budget simultaneously.

The project's broader lesson: even a small IoT deployment is a real optimization problem. That complexity is part of why IoT adoption has been more challenging and more gradual than the early hype suggested.

Current Challenges in IoT

Challenge Why It Matters
Interoperability and standardization Requires human agreement across vendors, not just a technical fix.
Software and integration complexity Each new device and platform increases combinatorial complexity.
RF and health concerns Growing 5G and wireless density raises questions about long-term exposure.
Privacy and security Especially for cameras and other visual sensors in public and home spaces.
Environmental impact E-waste from device turnover; radio-frequency saturation of the environment.
Automation of critical decisions Telehealth and similar applications raise questions about relying on software for high-stakes judgment.
The Social Side

IoT is not purely a technology discussion. Placement of sensors and cameras around people triggers real objections, regulation, and policy work. Dr. Dagtas emphasized that technology adoption and social acceptance move together, not independently.
