Case Study 1: Michigan Unemployment Fraud Algorithm (2013-2015)

A machine-learning system used by Michigan flagged roughly 20,000 residents for unemployment fraud, with reported error rates as high as 93%. The case emerged in the post-2008 recession environment, where unemployment was high and agencies were understaffed.

Impacts Discussed

  • Wage garnishments
  • Tax refund seizures
  • Credit damage and bankruptcies
  • Extended legal disputes and settlements
Core Lesson

Automated decisions in high-stakes systems require meaningful human oversight. Speed gains do not justify large-scale wrongful harm.

Case Study 2: Amazon Hiring Algorithm

Amazon trained a resume-screening model on approximately ten years of hiring data. Because historical data reflected male-dominated hiring patterns, the model learned to downgrade indicators associated with women, including resumes containing terms like "women's" (for example, women's chess club).

  • Historical training data encoded existing organizational bias.
  • Model output inherited and operationalized that bias.
  • Amazon eventually discontinued the system.
Modeling Principle

Biased input data produces biased model behavior. "Garbage in, garbage out" remains true even in sophisticated ML pipelines.

Case Study 3: COMPAS Recidivism Algorithm

The COMPAS risk score, used in criminal-justice settings, became a major fairness controversy after ProPublica's 2016 analysis reported disparate error rates by race. According to the class summary, the tool was about twice as likely to falsely flag Black defendants as high risk and about twice as likely to falsely clear white defendants.

Perspective Main Claim Ethics Implication
ProPublica Error rates were racially disparate in harmful ways. Equal overall accuracy can still mask unequal harm across groups.
Northpointe (Vendor) Overall predictive accuracy was comparable across groups. Different fairness metrics can produce conflicting conclusions.
Courts/Policy Context Admissibility and use of algorithmic scores remain debated. Transparency and accountability are crucial in public-sector AI.

General Lessons and Class Discussion

The closing discussion tied all three cases back to recurring ethical obligations for data scientists and system designers.

  1. Human oversight matters: automated tools should not replace accountable review in high-impact decisions.
  2. Training data carries values: models reflect the assumptions and biases embedded in source datasets.
  3. Fairness is multidimensional: a system can be "accurate" yet still unfair depending on which error metrics are examined.
  4. Transparency is essential: public trust depends on explainable models, especially in benefits, hiring, and criminal justice.
  5. Ethics is a professional duty: data science decisions create real material consequences for people and communities.
