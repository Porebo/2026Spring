AI, Deep Learning, and Large Language Model Foundations

This lecture connected the course’s earlier machine-learning content to modern AI systems, showing how deep neural networks, embeddings, transformers, and prompting strategies fit into the broader data science landscape.

Quick Summary

Dr. Pierce moved from program reminders into an overview of AI, deep learning, and large language models. The session explained how traditional classification problems scale into neural-network and deep-learning settings, how LLMs perform next-word prediction, why embeddings matter for text, where legal disputes over training data are emerging, and how prompting techniques and grounding methods improve practical use.

Where LLMs Fit

The lecture framed data science, AI, machine learning, deep learning, and large language models as overlapping layers. LLMs were presented as one specialized outcome of deeper neural-network advances rather than as a completely separate discipline.

From Simple Classification to Deep Learning

  • A music-classification example used tempo and energy to separate reggae and R&B songs.
  • The example showed why some problems can use simple linear boundaries while others need nonlinear decision boundaries.
  • As the number of variables, classes, and relationships grows, neural networks become more useful than simpler models.
  • Deep learning was explained as the extension from shallow neural networks to architectures with many hidden layers and much greater representational power.

Images and Text as Inputs

  • Images can be converted to pixel data and classified with neural-network architectures.
  • The lecture referenced CNN and RNN style models in the context of image and sequential data processing.
  • For text, sentiment analysis was used to show how sentences can be turned into numeric inputs and mapped to positive or negative labels.

Large Language Models and GPT

  • LLMs were described as systems trained to predict the next word in a sequence.
  • GPT was unpacked as Generative Pre-Trained Transformer.
  • The lecture connected transformer architecture, large-scale pretraining, instruction tuning, and reinforcement learning from human feedback.
  • Dr. Pierce emphasized that LLM output can be fluent and convincing without necessarily being correct.

Limits, Grounding, and Legal Tensions

  • One limitation of LLMs is currency: model training takes time, so newly emerging events may not be in the model’s built-in knowledge.
  • Search-augmented systems such as Bing Chat were used to illustrate how web search can ground answers with fresher information.
  • The lecture referenced the Encyclopedia Britannica and Merriam-Webster lawsuit against OpenAI as an example of copyright and training-data conflicts.
  • Students were reminded that LLMs generate plausible language, not guaranteed truth, which explains hallucination risk.

Embeddings and Prompt Engineering

  • Embeddings were introduced as numeric vector representations that let machine learning systems work with words, categories, and context.
  • One-hot encoding was presented as a simple baseline, but richer embeddings were emphasized because they preserve more useful relationships.
  • Zero-shot, few-shot, and chain-of-thought prompting were highlighted as practical ways to improve model responses.
  • Prompt structure, examples, and stepwise decomposition were framed as essential user-side skills.

Enterprise Applications

Examples included clinical documentation support, cybersecurity policy mapping, claims processing, customer-service automation, troubleshooting, market research, developer productivity, literature curation, and internal knowledge-base work.
