## Key Concepts
- **Minimalist symbolism:** spaghetti diagrams thrive on rectangles plus arrows, making them accessible but demanding disciplined annotations to avoid ambiguity.
- **Insight vs. prescription:** the maps brilliantly expose inefficiency yet offer no automated redesign; designers must propose layout experiments on top of the picture.
- **Health considerations:** shorter lines improve throughput, but sedentary risk grows if we remove all walking; analysts should explicitly capture both productivity and wellness metrics.

### Factory & Site Layouts
- **Mailroom case:** missing or misdirected arrows (e.g., Block 7) hide handoff gaps; keeping every edge directional enables credible pathway counts before proposing workstation swaps.
- **Battery plant before/after:** Daniel cited the 113 ft / 440 min travel baseline and a post-redesign cut to 33 ft / 130 min to show how the diagrams quantify gains when stations migrate closer.

### Weather, Web, and Migration Models
- **Hurricane tracks:** "spaghetti models" plot ensembles of predicted storm paths; overlaying many thin arrows highlights consensus corridors versus outliers.
- **Butterfly migration & wildlife tagging:** long-range movement traces follow the same aesthetic, making ecological journeys legible to non-experts.
- **Website navigation:** treating clicks as arrows between page rectangles clarifies where users loop or abandon, mirroring factory logic in digital space.

## Terminology Notes
- Cross-domain naming varies: manufacturing teams prefer "spaghetti diagram," meteorologists lean on "spaghetti plot/model," and UX researchers sometimes say "navigation chart." Document the context whenever you cite the technique.
