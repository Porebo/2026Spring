## Core Evaluation Framework
- A defensible evaluation must measure the right thing and do so in the right way.
- Measuring the wrong thing accurately is still useless.
- Measuring the right thing poorly is also useless.
- Daniel used classroom and used-car examples to show how easy it is to miss critical factors.

## Objectivist vs Interpretivist Evaluation
- **Objectivist view:** evaluation should be as objective, repeatable, and expert-driven as possible.
- **Interpretivist view:** evaluation is shaped by stakeholders, context, social judgment, and what people decide matters.
- The class compared quiz grading, unfair questions, and system requirements to show why both views matter.
- Takeaway: real evaluation plans should borrow strengths from both approaches.

## Success Criteria and Hidden Failure Modes
- A system is not automatically a success just because it meets its original requirements.
- Requirements may be incomplete, based on poor assumptions, or set by people who never use the system.
- Daniel connected this idea back to Workday as an example of a system that may satisfy official intentions while frustrating real users.

## Formative vs Summative Evaluation
- **Summative evaluation:** done at the end to judge overall outcome.
- **Formative evaluation:** done during development to improve the system while it is being built.
- Homework and quizzes can have both aspects, depending on whether revision or feedback loops are built in.
- Agile sprint retrospectives were presented as a strong example of formative evaluation in software development.

## Case Studies and Examples
- Personal nutrition tracking favored formative evaluation because frequent feedback helps adjust behavior sooner.
- A hypothetical rural health IT deployment showed how late, narrow evaluation can miss sustainability problems.
- Agile development was highlighted as a process that bakes evaluation into regular team cycles.

## Practical Takeaways
- Good evaluation depends on criteria, timing, evaluators, and stakeholder perspective.
- Human judgment is unavoidable in many real systems problems.
- Formative evaluation is especially useful when teams still have time to change course.
