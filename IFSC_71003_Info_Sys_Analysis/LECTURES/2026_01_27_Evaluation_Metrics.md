## Narrative of Activities

In this session, we explored the concept of **Quantitative Evaluation** for information systems, specifically focusing on websites. The lecture used university websites as a primary case study to demonstrate how subjective "quality" can be measured using objective, numerical data. We walked through the calculation of various metrics and discussed how different design philosophies (modern "flat" vs. traditional hierarchical) affect these scores.

## Teaching Points & Methodology

The core of the lecture was the **Information Quality Framework**, which breaks down site evaluation into six key dimensions.

### The 6 Dimensions of Website Quality

1. **Usefulness:** Measured by the ratio of successful sessions to total sessions.
2. **Usability:** Measured by the ratio of successful instructional tasks vs. help requests.
3. **Findability:** Measured by the percentage of time a site appears in the Top 10 search results for relevant queries.
4. **Accessibility:** Measured by compliance with scanners or the percentage of images containing `alt` text.
5. **Credibility:** Traditionally measured by the presence of a clear site structure (e.g., **Breadcrumbs**). *Note:* Modern designs often score 0% here despite being professional, highlighting a flaw in the metric.
6. **Value:** Measured by the percentage of essential materials (like faculty resources) available without password protection.
