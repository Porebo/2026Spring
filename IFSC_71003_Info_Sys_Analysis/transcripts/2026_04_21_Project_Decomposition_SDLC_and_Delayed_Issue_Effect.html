2. Evaluation Example Before the Main Lecture

Formative vs. summative

Before shifting into decomposition, Daniel briefly revisited the difference between formative and summative evaluation using a large failed military software project as an example.

His point was that a project can absorb years of work and billions of dollars, then still fail at the end if summative evaluation reveals unresolved core problems.

This example set up one of the lecture's broader concerns: how design choices and process structure affect project outcomes long before the final delivery moment.

3. Systems Analysis Goes Beyond Analysis

Field review
  • Daniel reviewed systems analysis as a field aimed at improving IT-enabled organizational processes.
  • He connected the course to process mapping, root cause analysis, prototyping, benchmarking, and cost-benefit thinking.
  • He emphasized that systems analysts do not stop at analysis in real practice; they also contribute to design and implementation.
  • The lecture therefore moved from the analysis phase into the design phase, especially system structure and decomposition.

4. Requirements, Specifications, and Design

Transition to design
  • A requirement states what the system should accomplish.
  • A specification makes that requirement more concrete and measurable.
  • Daniel framed this as a shift from the "why" and "what" of analysis into the "how" of design.
One practical message was that design is not just visual layout. It includes the architecture of the system and the structure of the work used to create it.

5. SDLC Models: Waterfall and Its Revisions

Lifecycle review
  • Daniel began with the classic waterfall model: analysis, design, implementation, testing, and deployment.
  • He argued that the simple one-way waterfall picture is misleading because real projects often need to revisit earlier stages.
  • More realistic diagrams include back arrows, verification steps, and repeated testing throughout the process.
  • He contrasted these structured approaches with the older build-and-fix model, where teams repeatedly patch problems without strong initial design.

6. Spiral, Unified Process, and Agile

Iterative alternatives
  • The spiral model re-expresses staged development while adding repeated verification and risk analysis.
  • The Unified Process was presented as iterative blocks that still contain specification, design, and implementation work.
  • Daniel used a skyscraper analogy: first establish the structural framework, then add usable features in later cycles.
  • Agile was discussed in a similar spirit, with short iterations that still perform requirements work, design, coding, and testing.
Even when a process is iterative, Daniel's message was that order still matters. Iteration is not the same as chaos.

7. Delayed Issue Effect

Classic software-engineering idea

Daniel introduced the delayed issue effect: the longstanding belief that defects become much more expensive to fix the later they are discovered in the lifecycle.

  • He tied this idea to Barry Boehm's 1981 work.
  • Classic charts often show defect-fix cost rising sharply from requirements to operation.
  • Older software-distribution models made the logic intuitive because post-release fixes could require large-scale replacement and customer communication.
Daniel's spreadsheet-company story illustrated why late fixes used to be so painful: defective physical copies had to be replaced after customers already had them.

8. Why the Delayed Issue Effect May Not Always Hold

Modern critique
  • Daniel contrasted Boehm's classic position with a 2016 study of 171 software projects that reported no evidence for a universal delayed issue effect.
  • He suggested that project size, development method, and deployment environment may all affect whether late fixes are truly much more expensive.
  • Cloud software was an important example: fixing a server-side issue in a service like Google Sheets is often cheaper than replacing physical software media.
The takeaway was not "Boehm was wrong" in every situation. It was that students should treat the delayed issue effect as context-dependent rather than as a universal law.

9. Fred Brooks and Project Decomposition

Design efficiency
  • Daniel used Fred Brooks and The Mythical Man-Month to explain why software effort cannot be understood as a simple count of person-months.
  • He reviewed four project types: fully decomposable, partially decomposable, non-decomposable, and difficult-to-decompose.
  • In fully decomposable work, adding people can reduce delivery time almost proportionally.
  • In partially decomposable work, coordination overhead reduces those gains.
  • In difficult-to-decompose work, too many people can actually slow the project down.

10. Good vs. Bad Decomposition

Structure matters
  • Daniel argued that decomposition should follow natural functional boundaries.
  • A CPU example showed that grouping meaningful components is far better than grouping raw low-level parts by type and trying to assemble them later.
  • He connected this to programming: a well-designed program groups logic into meaningful units rather than scattering related behavior across arbitrary categories.
  • He also connected it to websites, noting that both the overall site structure and the quality of each page affect usability.

11. Closing Review Questions

  1. The delayed issue effect is historically important, but modern evidence suggests it does not apply equally in every context.
  2. Adding people to a project only helps when the work can actually be decomposed well.
  3. Good decomposition means choosing boundaries that reduce interference and preserve meaningful structure.
