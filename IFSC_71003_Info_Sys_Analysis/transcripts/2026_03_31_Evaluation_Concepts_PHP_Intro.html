2. What Is Evaluation?

00:00–10:00

Definition Critique

Dictionary.com: "An act or instance of evaluating or appraising." This definition is circular — it defines evaluation using a form of the same word. The word "appraising" provides the escape route.

A better definition: a systematic quality assessment.

More Than Pass/Fail

Evaluation is not just whether something works — it is how well it works. Binary (works/doesn't work) is the simplest form. Richer evaluation uses a scale:

  • Categorical scale: A, B, C, D, F — discrete labeled categories
  • Numerical scale: a continuous spectrum (e.g., 0–100 score)
  • Pass/Fail: the simplest binary — a valid but limited form of evaluation
Formalized evaluation: In professional or course contexts, evaluation becomes explicit — weighted percentages, documented criteria, transparent protocols. Knowing the rubric in advance is standard in school; in the real world, it often isn't.

3. Key Questions in Any Evaluation

04:00–18:00

Before an evaluation can be valid, several foundational decisions must be made explicitly:

QuestionExamples
What concerns are being evaluated?In this course: spelling is not graded. An IT person evaluates connectivity; an interior decorator evaluates warmth and color scheme. The rack of exposed wires in EIT classrooms was a deliberate design choice — construction management students benefit from seeing real infrastructure.
Whose concerns are being served?Grades serve: the student? The instructor? The university? Parents? Employers? Someone has to decide. The answer shapes what gets measured.
How rigorous is the standard?90% threshold for an A vs. 10% threshold — same grading scheme, radically different standards. Same applies to DFDs, FMEAs, or any evaluated artifact.
What factors are included?Midterm + final exams? Homeworks? Class participation? Teacher's judgment? Each weights different skills differently.
Who does the measuring?TA, instructor, expert, external lab, the person being evaluated? Each brings different biases.
What if the appraisal is uncertain?Accept the fishbone diagram as-is, or request more detail? Different philosophies; different risk tolerances.
Unstated or wrong criteria: Evaluation criteria are often assumed rather than declared. An interior decorator probably won't evaluate wiring, but if connectivity is what matters, the evaluation is useless. Criteria can even be illegitimate — wrong by any reasonable standard.

4. Evaluation Is Both Objective and Interpretive

18:00–28:00

Evaluation is not purely data-driven. Two components always coexist:

  • Objective: collecting data, computing numbers, checking boxes.
  • Interpretive: deciding what data to collect, why those metrics, who decides what counts, and how to interpret ambiguous results.

Example: Likert-scale course evaluations (1–5 agree/disagree). Two students who feel exactly the same way may still circle different numbers. The number is not purely objective.

True or false: "Evaluation in systems analysis is straightforward once you have the right data."

False. Having data is necessary but not sufficient. What is the "right" data? An interior decorator and an IT person define it completely differently. The word straightforward is the problem — you still need to decide what the data means, whether it was the right data to collect, whose agenda shaped the data collection, and how to interpret the results.

5. Stakeholders

20:00–24:00

A stakeholder is anyone who has a stake in the outcome — who cares what the evaluation finds. Stakeholders include:

  • Users — the people who operate the system daily
  • Investors — who care about financial return (not always aligned with quality)
  • Engineers/IT staff — who built the system; their professional reputation is on the line
  • The organization — which bears operational risk
Can two stakeholders reach opposite conclusions about whether the same system is working? Yes. They may have different data, different success criteria, and different standards. Daniel's example: "Some people think Blackboard is working. I personally have my doubts."

Early tech companies like Google illustrate this: in early stages, user satisfaction drives the product. Once investors demand returns, quality sometimes yields to ad revenue or monetization — different stakeholders taking precedence at different company life-cycle stages.

6. Five Dimensions of Evaluation

27:00–46:00

Just as information quality has multiple dimensions (timeliness, correctness, completeness), evaluation can be applied along five key dimensions. Weakness in any one compromises the entire evaluation — even if the other four are done perfectly.

#DimensionKey QuestionFailure Example
1Overall systemDoes it work? How well? Rate it.System operates but too slowly for real-time use
2CriteriaWhat does "works" mean? What standards apply?Measuring uptime and new accounts instead of sustainability and staff capacity
3Analysis processWas the evaluation conducted soundly?Mars Climate Orbiter: subsystems evaluated in isolation; interface units mismatch undetected
4EvaluatorsWho evaluated? Are they independent and qualified?A professor placed in his own promotion evaluation chain; Space Shuttle engineers overridden by management pressure
5TimingWhen was the evaluation done — during the project or after?Evaluating only at project end; problems discovered too late to correct

Classroom Scenario: The Exposed Wires

The exposed ductwork, wiring, and steel beams visible in EIT classrooms were an intentional choice. Construction management students benefit from seeing real building infrastructure; the university also saved money on finishes. Evaluation of the classroom differs by evaluator perspective — an interior decorator focuses on warmth and aesthetics; an IT person focuses on connectivity; a facilities manager focuses on code compliance (the fuzzy insulation on steel beams is fire-code required — a severe fire can weaken or melt steel, so insulation is now standard).

Mars Climate Orbiter (1999): One contractor used pound-force·seconds; NASA assumed Newton·seconds. Every subsystem passed its own evaluation. Dimension 3 (analysis process) failed: no one verified the unit convention at the software interface. The $125M spacecraft entered the Martian atmosphere at the wrong angle and was destroyed.

Class Discussion Questions

  • Q: Which dimension applies when evaluating project success vs. tracking project progress?
    A: Dimension 5 (Timing) — the distinction is simply when you evaluate. Post-project = summative; ongoing = formative.
  • Q: Which dimension applies when the goal is to anticipate cost/time overruns as early as possible?
    A: Dimension 5 (Timing) — requires evaluating early and continuously while the project is underway.
  • Q (T/F): If a system meets all its stated goals, the evaluation is complete.
    A: False. The stated goals may be wrong (Dim 2); the analysis process may be flawed (Dim 3); evaluators may be biased (Dim 4); and new criteria may emerge post-deployment.

7. Evaluator Independence

39:00–50:00

Evaluators can be compromised in several ways:

  • Organizational pressure: An evaluator hired by the project's sponsor may feel — consciously or unconsciously — pressure to deliver a favorable result.
  • Employment risk: No one wants to lose their job for giving an accurate-but-unflattering evaluation.
  • Conflict of interest: Placing a person in their own evaluation chain is inappropriate in formal settings. (Daniel declined to participate in a promotion review where the candidate was in the evaluation hierarchy.)
Space Shuttle Challenger (1986): Engineers knew the O-ring rubber seals would fail in cold temperatures. Under severe real-time management pressure, engineers approved the launch anyway. The organizational pressure on the evaluators overrode valid technical judgment — and the shuttle broke apart 73 seconds after launch, killing all seven crew members.
Solution: third-party independent evaluation. Underwriters Laboratories (UL) certifies electrical appliances independently from the manufacturers. For IT systems, an external testing firm has no incentive to please the client — this is why independent audits, penetration tests, and usability studies by external labs are industry best practice.

8. Evaluation Failure Case Studies

46:00–63:00

Case 1 — Personal Life: Study Habits

A student evaluates their study habits by counting hours studied. They budget more hours; grades don't improve.

What went wrong: Hours is an easy number to measure, but quality and method of study — not quantity — was the actual bottleneck. The criteria (Dimension 2) were wrong.

What was measuredBetter alternatives
Hours studiedLearning goals set and met per session
—Quiz/test score improvement over time
—Method variation experiments (different study techniques → compare outcomes)
—Tracking specific previous failure areas and measuring targeted improvement

Case 2 — Mobile Health App in a Developing Country

A health app is deployed to rural clinics in a developing country. Evaluators measure uptime and new account registrations. Both look excellent — the project is declared a success. The project later collapses.

What was measuredWhat should have been measured
System uptimeAvailability of local IT support staff to maintain the system
New account registrationsSustainable staff training plan and training capacity
(nothing)Integration with existing paper-based workflows
(nothing)Long-term funding sustainability after initial grant period
Dimension 2 failure (Criteria): Uptime and registration are easy to measure. Sustainability is hard to measure. But sustainability is what actually matters for a rural clinic — and it wasn't evaluated at all. This pattern recurs frequently in health IT projects in developing countries.

Case 3 — Enterprise IT: Workday at UALR

The University of Arkansas System mandated the Workday ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) system across all campuses. Years later, UALR is still struggling with the transition.

What was apparently not evaluatedConsequence
No pilot campus tested before system-wide mandateNo early-warning evaluation before full rollout
No staff/faculty usability testingPrimary users had no input; unexpected usability problems surfaced only after full deployment
No structured training assessmentKnowledgeable staff too busy firefighting to help colleagues; training pipeline collapsed
No salary/staffing impact analysisNew system complexity quietly raised the expertise bar — and therefore the compensation expectations — for staff positions
Dimensions missed: Dimension 3 (no independent usability evaluation before rollout), Dimension 4 (decision-makers appeared to have no direct hands-on experience with the system), Dimension 5 (no formative evaluation during rollout to catch and correct problems early).
"Evaluation is not just about evaluating — it's what you evaluate, who the evaluators are, and when you evaluate."
— Daniel Berleant

9. Key Takeaways & Next Class

Summary
  • Evaluation is multidimensional — all five dimensions must pass; one weak link degrades the whole.
  • Having the right data is necessary but not sufficient — who decides what's "right" is the harder question.
  • Evaluators must be independent; organizational pressure is a persistent threat to valid evaluation.
  • Wrong criteria are the most common failure mode — a system can pass every test and still fail in the real world.
  • Formative evaluation (ongoing) is almost always more valuable than summative evaluation (after the fact).
