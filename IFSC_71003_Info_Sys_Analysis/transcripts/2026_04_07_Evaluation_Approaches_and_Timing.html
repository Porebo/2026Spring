2. The Only Defensible Evaluation

Used-car and classroom examples

Daniel framed the session around one central principle: a valid evaluation must measure the right thing and do so the right way.

What HappensWhy It Fails
Measure the wrong thing accuratelyPrecise but irrelevant results are still useless.
Measure the right thing badlyThe target is correct, but the method corrupts the result.
Measure the wrong thing badlyWorst case: no trustworthy information at all.
Measure the right thing correctlyThe only defensible combination.

His examples included used-car inspections, tire quality, and even classroom comfort. The point was that evaluation errors often come from overlooking the important dimension, not merely from bad arithmetic.

3. Objectivist vs Interpretivist Views

Main theory segment

Objectivist View

  • Evaluation should be objective, repeatable, and produce the same answer no matter who performs it.
  • Quiz grading with a clear answer key was used as the model example.
  • This approach values rigor, consistency, and expert procedure.

Interpretivist View

  • Evaluation is shaped by judgment, context, and social decisions about what matters.
  • Even a perfectly graded quiz still reflects human choices about what to ask in the first place.
  • Stakeholders, institutions, and local priorities influence what gets measured and how success is defined.
Real systems evaluation is never just about collecting data. It also depends on who chose the criteria, who applies them, and whose concerns the evaluation is supposed to serve.

4. Requirements Are Not Automatically Truth

Workday and stakeholder examples

Daniel challenged the idea that a system is successful simply because it performs "as designed." That standard can fail when the design itself is incomplete, unrealistic, or imposed by people who never actually use the system.

  • Requirements documents may reflect negotiation rather than wisdom.
  • Different evaluators may prioritize different dimensions of quality.
  • A system can satisfy official specifications and still frustrate real users.
Workday returned as a cautionary example: leaders can define requirements and purchasing decisions without really understanding day-to-day user experience.

5. Formative vs Summative Evaluation

Class discussion and examples
TypeWhen It HappensMain Purpose
FormativeDuring the project or learning processImprove performance while there is still time to change course
SummativeAt the endJudge the final outcome
  • A final course grade is strongly summative.
  • Homework with meaningful feedback can have a formative role, especially early in the semester.
  • Promotion decisions are usually summative.
  • Tracking project time, budget, and sprint quality is formative.

6. Case Studies and Why Timing Matters

Later examples
  • A nutrition-tracking example showed how day-to-day evidence can guide better decisions than a single end-of-semester judgment.
  • A hypothetical rural health IT deployment showed how a late success declaration can hide sustainability failures.
  • Agile sprint retrospectives were presented as a textbook example of formative evaluation built directly into the software process.
Daniel's strongest conclusion was that organizations need both types of evaluation, but formative evaluation is what helps teams improve while the work is still alive.

7. Key Takeaways

  1. Good evaluation depends on both the target and the method.
  2. Human judgment cannot be removed entirely from real systems evaluation.
  3. Requirements and official criteria may themselves need evaluation.
  4. Formative and summative evaluation serve different purposes and should not be confused.
  5. Agile development is valuable partly because it formalizes recurring formative evaluation.
